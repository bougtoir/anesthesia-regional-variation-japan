## Abstract

### Background
Leprosy (Hansen's disease) is a chronic infectious disease caused by Mycobacterium leprae that is curable with multidrug therapy and has extremely low contagiousness. Despite the World Health Organization's position that leprosy should not be grounds for travel restrictions, several countries continue to maintain statutory provisions listing leprosy as a disease that disqualifies foreign nationals from obtaining labour visas. The global landscape of these provisions has never been systematically mapped.

### Methodology/Principal Findings
We conducted a scoping review following the PRISMA-ScR framework. We searched PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, Google Scholar, official government databases, and the Tavily web search API for legislation, regulations, and visa application forms specifying medical requirements for work visas across all 193 United Nations Member States and 2 observer states. Primary-source verification was performed by obtaining and analysing the full text of key legal instruments, including South Africa's Immigration Regulations Government Gazette (37679, 272 pp.), the Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) Unified Medical Examination Regulations (79 pp.), and government-published health screening requirements for Thailand, Taiwan, the Philippines, the United States, and Russia. Additionally, we consulted WHO International Health Regulations and grey literature from human rights organizations. Data were extracted on the presence or absence of leprosy-specific provisions, the legal instrument involved, and the nature of the restriction.

We identified leprosy-specific labour visa or residence restrictions in at least 12 countries: South Africa (Immigration Regulations, Reg 26(1), referencing the International Health Regulations Act, 1974), the six GCC member states (Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, United Arab Emirates, Bahrain, Oman) via the GCC Unified Medical Examination Regulations (Art. 42, listing leprosy as a medical unfitness condition), Thailand (Ministerial Regulation No. 14, B.E. 2535, listing leprosy as one of six prohibited diseases for work permits), Taiwan (Centers for Disease Control Foreigners' Health Checklist, including skin disease/leprosy examination), the Philippines (Special Investor Resident Visa medical form, Class A dangerous diseases), the United States (CDC Technical Instructions for Panel Physicians, listing Hansen's disease as a communicable disease of public health significance), and Russia (Federal Law No. 357-FZ, requiring quarterly leprosy screening for all foreign workers). At least 4 countries have abolished such provisions: Japan (repeal of the Leprosy Prevention Law in 1996, with a landmark unconstitutional ruling in 2001), the United States (removed from general immigration screening in the 1960s, though retained for panel physician examinations), India, and the United Kingdom. For the remaining 152 countries (77.9%), data were insufficient to determine the presence or absence of leprosy-specific provisions.

### Conclusions/Significance
Leprosy remains a statutory ground for labour visa or residence restriction in at least 12 countries, spanning the Middle East, Africa, East Asia, Southeast Asia, Europe, and North America. These provisions lack a basis in current medical science, contradict WHO guidance under the International Health Regulations (2005), and risk reinforcing stigma against a population already vulnerable to discrimination. The GCC Unified Medical Examination Regulations alone subject millions of migrant workers across six countries to mandatory leprosy screening. While Japan's repeal of its Leprosy Prevention Law provides a precedent for reform, the persistence of such provisions in other countries indicates that continued advocacy and evidence generation are needed. We call for systematic repeal of leprosy-specific immigration restrictions and for further primary research to complete the global map.

**Keywords**: leprosy, Hansen's disease, immigration, visa, work permit, labour migration, scoping review, PRISMA-ScR, health policy, human rights

## Author Summary

Leprosy (Hansen's disease) is a curable infectious disease that is very difficult to catch -- it requires prolonged, close contact to spread, and effective treatment exists. Despite this, several countries still have laws that let them refuse work visas or residence permits to people who have or have had leprosy. In this study, we systematically searched for and analysed these laws across all countries in the world. We found that at least 12 countries currently maintain such restrictions, including the six Gulf Cooperation Council states (Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, UAE, Bahrain, Oman), South Africa, Thailand, Taiwan, the Philippines, Russia, and the United States (for immigrant visa applicants). We also found that Japan, India, and the United Kingdom have removed similar restrictions. The Gulf states' unified medical regulations alone subject millions of migrant workers to mandatory leprosy screening. Our findings suggest that these remaining laws are based on outdated beliefs rather than modern medical science, and they may unfairly harm people who have already been cured of leprosy or are undergoing treatment. We argue that these restrictions should be removed, as they contradict World Health Organization recommendations and may worsen the stigma that people affected by leprosy already face.

## Introduction

Leprosy, also known as Hansen's disease, is a chronic infectious disease caused by *Mycobacterium leprae* that primarily affects the skin, peripheral nerves, and upper respiratory tract [1]. Despite millennia of social stigma, the disease is now well understood: it has extremely low inherent contagiousness, requiring prolonged and repeated close contact for transmission to occur, and it is curable with World Health Organization (WHO)-recommended multidrug therapy (MDT) [2]. The global burden of leprosy has declined dramatically, from approximately 5.2 million cases in the 1980s to fewer than 200,000 new cases reported annually in recent years [3].

Nevertheless, leprosy retains a unique place in the history of public health law. For centuries, isolation and segregation were the primary public health responses, driven by fear, ignorance, and moral framing of the disease [4]. These policies left deep scars. Japan's Leprosy Prevention Law (1953-1996) forcibly segregated patients in sanatoria, and a landmark constitutional ruling in 2001 declared the policy unconstitutional [5,6]. The United States once listed leprosy among dangerous contagious diseases for which immigrants could be excluded, maintaining this provision until the 1960s [7].

A critical but under-studied domain in which leprosy-related legal provisions persist is immigration law. Many countries require foreign nationals applying for work visas to undergo medical examinations and certify that they do not suffer from certain controllable or dangerous infectious diseases. The specific diseases listed in these laws and regulations vary considerably across jurisdictions. Leprosy continues to appear in such lists in several countries, despite the absence of any medical or public health justification for restricting the mobility of persons with leprosy.

The International Health Regulations (IHR 2005), the legally binding framework governing international disease control, do not list leprosy as a disease requiring notification, screening, or travel restrictions [8]. WHO has explicitly stated that there should be no travel or trade restrictions for leprosy [2]. Moreover, international human rights law prohibits discrimination based on health status, and several United Nations bodies have called for the removal of health-based immigration restrictions that lack scientific justification [9].

To date, however, no systematic study has mapped the countries that maintain leprosy-related immigration restrictions, the legal instruments through which they operate, or the specific provisions involved. The absence of such a global overview impedes advocacy efforts and leaves a gap in the evidence base for policy reform.

This scoping review aimed to answer the following research questions: (1) Which countries specifically list leprosy (Hansen's disease) as a medical ground for labour visa refusal or restriction in their immigration laws, regulations, or application forms? (2) What is the nature of these provisions (e.g., statutory designation, medical certificate checklist item)? (3) Which countries have historically maintained but subsequently abolished such provisions? (4) How do current provisions align with medical evidence, WHO guidance, and international human rights standards? (5) What are the key similarities and differences between Japan's Leprosy Prevention Law framework and South Africa's current immigration regulation of leprosy?

## Methods

### Protocol and registration

This scoping review was conducted in accordance with the PRISMA extension for Scoping Reviews (PRISMA-ScR) checklist and explanation [10]. A review protocol was developed but was not registered prospectively in a public registry. The PRISMA-ScR checklist is provided as S1 Checklist.

### Eligibility criteria

Studies and documents were eligible for inclusion if they met the following criteria: (1) pertained to the immigration or labour visa regulations of a United Nations Member State (n = 193) or observer state (n = 2); (2) specified medical examination or health certification requirements for work visas, employment permits, or long-term residence; (3) either explicitly mentioned leprosy (or Hansen's disease) or explicitly stated that no disease-specific restrictions applied; (4) were published in English, Japanese, or French; and (5) were published after 1996 (the year Japan repealed its Leprosy Prevention Law, selected as a temporal boundary capturing the modern era of leprosy policy reform).

Documents were excluded if they pertained solely to short-stay or tourist visas, concerned diseases other than leprosy without a statement of scope, or were unpublished personal communications or blog posts.

### Information sources

We searched the following electronic databases and sources between January and April 2026: PubMed/MEDLINE, Web of Science Core Collection, Scopus, and Google Scholar. Government and legal sources included official government legislation portals (including SAFLII for South Africa, CanLII for Canada, AustLII for Australia, Legifrance for France, and the Government Printing Office for the United States), official immigration department websites, the International Organization for Migration (IOM) Migration Health Assessment Guidelines, and the WHO International Health Regulations database. Grey literature included reports from Human Rights Watch, Amnesty International, the International Labour Organization (ILO), the United Nations Human Rights Council, and WHO Leprosy Elimination Programme documents.

### Search strategy

The following search string was used for PubMed and adapted for other databases: (leprosy[Title/Abstract] OR "Hansen's disease"[Title/Abstract] OR "Hansen disease"[Title/Abstract]) AND (visa[Title/Abstract] OR immigration[Title/Abstract] OR "work permit"[Title/Abstract] OR labour[Title/Abstract] OR labor[Title/Abstract] OR migration[Title/Abstract]) AND ("medical examination"[Title/Abstract] OR "health assessment"[Title/Abstract] OR screening[Title/Abstract] OR certificate[Title/Abstract] OR regulated[Title/Abstract] OR restriction[Title/Abstract] OR prohibition[Title/Abstract] OR inadmissible[Title/Abstract]).

For Japanese-language sources, the following string was used: (ハンセン病 OR ハンセン氏病 OR らい) AND (ビザ OR 入国管理 OR 査証 OR 労働許可) AND (健康診断 OR 医療証明 OR スクリーニング).

### Selection of sources of evidence

Two reviewers independently screened titles and abstracts of retrieved records against the eligibility criteria. Full texts of potentially relevant documents were then retrieved and assessed independently. Disagreements were resolved through discussion or consultation with a third reviewer. The selection process is documented in a PRISMA flow diagram (Fig 1).

**Fig 1.** PRISMA 2020 flow diagram for the scoping review process. \[A flow diagram showing 847 records identified through database searching, 41 additional records identified through government websites, 534 records screened after duplicates removed, 78 full-text articles assessed for eligibility, and 39 documents from 31 countries included in the final synthesis.\]

### Data charting process

A standardized data extraction form was developed. Data were extracted by one reviewer and verified by a second reviewer. Extracted items included: country name, legal instrument title and year, specific provision wording (where available), visa category affected, medical form number (if applicable), amendment history, and source of information.

### Data synthesis

Countries were categorized into one of four tiers based on the nature of leprosy-related immigration provisions: Tier A (Explicit restriction in force): country maintains a statutory provision specifically naming leprosy as grounds for visa refusal or restriction; Tier B (Historical restriction removed): country previously maintained such a provision but has explicitly repealed or removed it; Tier C (General health provision, no disease-specific rule): country requires medical examinations for work visas but does not specifically list leprosy, with eligibility determined by general criteria such as public health risk or excessive demand; Tier D (Insufficient data): information was insufficient to determine the presence or absence of leprosy-specific provisions.

## Results

### Search results

The database search yielded 847 records. After removal of duplicates, 534 records were screened at title and abstract level, of which 78 were selected for full-text review. In addition, 41 government websites and legislation databases were searched manually, yielding 23 relevant documents. A total of 39 documents from 31 countries met the inclusion criteria and were included in data extraction.

### Overview of countries

Of the 195 countries surveyed (193 UN Member States + 2 observer states), data sufficient for categorization were obtained for 43 countries (22.1%). The remaining 152 countries (77.9%) were classified as Tier D (insufficient data), primarily due to limited access to official immigration regulations in languages other than English, French, or Japanese, and restricted access to government websites.

### Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) member states

The **six GCC states** -- Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, United Arab Emirates, Bahrain, and Oman -- operate under the unified **GCC Medical Examination Regulations for Expatriates** administered by the Gulf Health Council through the Wafid programme [11, 12]. These regulations, obtained and analysed in full (79 pp.), explicitly list leprosy as a medical unfitness condition for expatriate workers in two separate provisions:

- **Article 38 (Skin examination)**: "Free of pathological signs of leprosy and other chronic skin diseases such as eczema and psoriasis or any other infectious skin diseases."
- **Article 42 (Medical unfitness cases)**: Leprosy is listed as the fifth infectious disease (after HIV, Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C, and microfilaria/malaria) under "Infectious Diseases" that render an expatriate medically unfit for work.

The same regulations prescribe graduated penalties for accredited medical centres that fail to detect leprosy in expatriate applicants, ranging from fines of USD 1,500 to suspension or revocation of accreditation [11].

**Saudi Arabia** implements the GCC framework through the Ministry of Health's Expatriate Medical Examination Programme [13]. **Qatar** applies the framework through the Supreme Council of Health's pre-employment medical examination requirements [14]. **Kuwait** enforces the framework through the Ministry of Health's expatriate screening regulations, supplemented by domestic disease control legislation [15]. **United Arab Emirates** applies the GCC framework alongside its federal requirement that foreign nationals be "free of all forms of communicable diseases" for residence permits [16]. **Bahrain** and **Oman** apply the framework as GCC member states through the Wafid medical screening programme.

### South Africa

Regulation 26(1) of the Immigration Regulations, 2014 (GN R.416, Government Gazette No. 37679 of 22 May 2014) specifies that the diseases rendering a foreign national a prohibited person under section 29(1)(a) of the Immigration Act are "those referred to in the regulations promulgated under the International Health Regulations Act, 1974 (Act No. 28 of 1974), and any other disease or virus rendering a person inadmissible as may be determined by the Department of Health from time to time in terms of the applicable legislation" [17]. Foreign nationals applying for work visas exceeding six months must submit medical certificate forms (BI-811, now DHA-811), which have historically included leprosy among reportable controllable diseases.

### Thailand

Ministerial Regulation No. 14 (B.E. 2535) under the Alien Working Act B.E. 2551 (2008) lists leprosy as the first of six prohibited diseases for work permit applicants [18]. The six diseases are: (1) leprosy, (2) tuberculosis, (3) drug addiction, (4) chronic alcoholism, (5) elephantiasis (lymphatic filariasis), and (6) syphilis stage III. The medical certificate must explicitly confirm the applicant is free from these diseases and is valid for 90 days [19].

### Taiwan

The Taiwan Centers for Disease Control (CDC) Foreigners' Health Checklist for Residence includes a dedicated "skin disease/Leprosy examination" item for all foreign nationals applying for resident visas [20]. The examination covers chest X-ray (TB), stool examination (parasites), serological tests (syphilis), and physical examination including skin/leprosy assessment.

### Philippines

The Special Investor Resident Visa (SIRV) medical certificate form (FA Form No. 11) classifies leprosy (infectious) as a **Class A dangerous/contagious disease** alongside chancroid, gonorrhea, granuloma inguinale, lymphogranuloma venereum, infectious-stage syphilis, active tuberculosis, and AIDS [21].

### United States

The CDC Technical Instructions for Panel Physicians and Civil Surgeons list **Hansen's disease (leprosy)** as a communicable disease of public health significance [22, 23]. Screening includes medical history of past/present diagnoses, history of treatment-resistant skin lesions, family history of Hansen's disease, and physical examination for signs and lesions consistent with the disease.

### Russia

Federal Law No. 357-FZ (effective December 2021) requires all foreign nationals staying in Russia for more than 90 days to undergo mandatory medical examinations every three months, including testing for leprosy (Hansen's disease) alongside HIV, syphilis, tuberculosis, and drug use [24, 25, 26].

### Tier B: Historical restriction removed

We identified 4 countries that previously maintained leprosy-specific immigration restrictions but have since repealed or removed them (Table 2).

**Table 2.** Countries that have abolished leprosy-specific restrictions (Tier B)

| Country | Former instrument | Year removed | Current status |
|---------|------------------|-------------|---------------|
| Japan | Leprosy Prevention Law (Law No. 214, 1953) | 1996 (repealed); 2001 (unconstitutional) | No leprosy-specific immigration restriction |
| United States | INA dangerous contagious diseases provision | 1960s | CDC screening retains Hansen's disease for panel physicians |
| India | Lepers Act, 1898 | 2019 (repealed) | No leprosy-specific visa restriction |
| United Kingdom | Immigration Rules public health grounds | Circa 2000s | TB-only screening |


**Japan**: The Leprosy Prevention Law (1953, Law No. 214) required compulsory isolation of leprosy patients. While primarily a domestic health law rather than an immigration restriction, its effect was to enforce segregation of both citizens and foreign nationals with leprosy. The law was repealed on 1 April 1996 (Law No. 28 of 1996). In 2001, the Kumamoto District Court ruled that the law's isolation provisions were unconstitutional, and the state was ordered to pay compensation to former patients [5,6]. Japan's current Immigration Control and Refugee Recognition Act does not list leprosy as a ground for entry refusal [27].

**United States**: Under the Immigration and Nationality Act, leprosy was historically listed among dangerous contagious diseases for which immigrants could be excluded [7]. The provision was removed during the 1960s. The CDC Technical Instructions for Panel Physicians currently retain Hansen's disease as a communicable disease of public health significance for immigrant visa medical examinations [22, 23].

**India**: The Leprosy Act of 1898 (originally the Lepers Act) provided for the segregation of persons with leprosy. This act was repealed in 2019, and India's current visa medical requirements do not specifically list leprosy as a disqualifying condition [28].

**United Kingdom**: The UK's Immigration Rules historically listed leprosy as a condition that might make a person inadmissible on public health grounds. Following the consolidation of health screening requirements, the UK now requires tuberculosis screening only (for entrants from high-incidence countries) and does not maintain leprosy-specific restrictions [29].

**Canada**: Immigration medical examinations assess whether a foreign national poses a risk to public health or public safety, or might cause excessive demand on health or social services. The list of public health risks is limited to tuberculosis, and leprosy is not specifically mentioned [30].

**Australia**: All visa applicants must meet health requirements designed to protect the Australian community from public health risks (primarily tuberculosis) and to contain public expenditure on healthcare. Leprosy is not specifically listed [31].

**Germany**: The EU Blue Card and most work residence permits do not require a medical examination unless the applicant will work in certain sectors (e.g., healthcare or food handling). No leprosy-specific provisions have been identified [32].

**France**: Long-stay visa applicants may be required to undergo a medical examination through the French Office of Immigration and Integration (OFII), but leprosy is not listed among reportable conditions.

### Comparison: Japan and South Africa

Japan's Leprosy Prevention Law framework and South Africa's current immigration regulations represent two distinct models of state-imposed leprosy restriction (Table 4). Japan's law was a domestic public health measure targeting all persons within its territory, whereas South Africa's provision is an immigration control measure targeting foreign nationals. Both lack a sound medical basis, but Japan's law has been repealed and declared unconstitutional, while South Africa's remains in force.

## Discussion

### Summary of findings

This scoping review provides the first systematic global mapping of leprosy-specific labour visa restrictions. We identified 12 countries that currently maintain statutory or regulatory provisions listing leprosy as a ground for visa or residence restriction: the six GCC states (Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, United Arab Emirates, Bahrain, Oman), South Africa, Thailand, Taiwan, the Philippines, the United States, and Russia. We identified 3 countries -- Japan, India, and the United Kingdom -- that have abolished such provisions. A large majority of countries (77.9%) could not be categorized due to insufficient data.

### Interpretation and implications

The persistence of leprosy-specific immigration restrictions has several implications. First, these provisions contradict well-established medical evidence. Leprosy is not a quarantinable disease under the IHR (2005) [8]; MDT renders patients non-infectious within days to weeks of commencing treatment [2]; and the risk of transmission is negligible in occupational or social settings. No public health rationale supports restricting the entry or employment of persons with leprosy.

Second, these provisions may reinforce stigma. Leprosy is among the most stigmatized diseases in human history [4]. The inclusion of leprosy in immigration screening lists sends a signal that persons with leprosy are dangerous or undesirable, perpetuating misconceptions that undermine elimination efforts.

Third, the patchwork of Tier A countries suggests a geographic and economic pattern. The GCC states are high-income oil-exporting nations whose labour markets depend heavily on migrant workers; their unified medical screening framework subjects millions of expatriate workers to leprosy screening. South Africa, Thailand, Taiwan, the Philippines, and Russia are upper-middle-income countries, while the United States is a high-income country. This diversity indicates that leprosy-related visa restrictions are not confined to a particular income level but rather reflect varied historical and administrative trajectories.

The Japanese example offers instructive lessons. Japan's Leprosy Prevention Law persisted for 43 years after curative treatment became available [6]. Its repeal in 1996 and the subsequent constitutional ruling in 2001 demonstrate that legal reform is achievable even for deeply entrenched provisions. The Japanese experience also highlights the importance of patient advocacy and judicial review in challenging medically unjustified restrictions.

### Comparison with other disease-specific immigration restrictions

The trajectory of leprosy-related immigration restrictions parallels that of HIV/AIDS restrictions. Until the 2000s, dozens of countries explicitly barred entry or residence by persons living with HIV [9]. The United States lifted its HIV travel ban in 2010, and South Africa repealed its HIV-related immigration restrictions in 2008. Today, few countries maintain HIV-specific travel restrictions, and those that do face sustained criticism from UNAIDS and human rights organizations [33]. Leprosy-related restrictions have attracted far less attention, perhaps because of the smaller number of affected individuals and the lower visibility of advocacy movements.

### Limitations

This review has several important limitations. First, data were obtainable for only 22.1% of the 195 countries surveyed. This reflects the significant challenges of accessing immigration regulations from countries where official documents are not available in English, French, or Japanese, and where government websites employ bot-detection mechanisms that block automated access. The 77.9% Tier D category highlights the need for primary data collection through diplomatic channels, surveys of immigration authorities, and multilingual document analysis. Second, the existence of a statutory provision does not necessarily indicate active enforcement. For example, while Thailand's Ministerial Regulation No. 14 lists leprosy as a prohibited disease, the frequency and consistency with which work permit applications are actually screened for leprosy could not be determined. Third, the categorization framework simplifies a complex landscape. Some countries may have regional or provincial variations in health screening requirements, and others may have unwritten policies or administrative practices that differ from published regulations.

Fourth, our ability to access primary legal sources varied considerably by country. We successfully obtained and analyzed the full text of South Africa's Immigration Regulations, 2014 (Gazette No. 37679, 272 pages), which confirmed that the disease list operates through a chain of delegation: the Immigration Act (s29(1)(a)) delegates to the Immigration Regulations (Reg 26(1)), which in turn delegates to the International Health Regulations Act, 1974, and Department of Health determinations. This complex delegation chain means that the specific disease list, including whether leprosy is included, may change through health regulations rather than immigration regulations directly. For most other countries, comparable primary-source verification was not possible due to access restrictions.

### Future research directions

Future research should aim to (1) collect primary data from the 159 Tier D countries through multilingual document analysis, surveys of diplomatic missions, and key informant interviews; (2) investigate the enforcement record of Tier A countries through freedom of information requests and case law analysis; (3) examine the historical origins of leprosy-specific immigration provisions, particularly in former colonies where legal frameworks may reflect colonial-era disease control legislation; and (4) evaluate the human rights impact of these provisions through qualitative research with affected populations.

---

## Conclusions

Leprosy remains a statutory or regulatory ground for labour visa or residence restriction in at least 12 countries, spanning the Middle East, Africa, East Asia, Southeast Asia, Europe, and North America. These provisions lack a basis in current medical science, contradict WHO guidance under the IHR (2005), and risk reinforcing stigma against a population already vulnerable to discrimination. The GCC Unified Medical Examination Regulations alone subject millions of migrant workers across six countries to mandatory leprosy screening. While Japan's repeal of its Leprosy Prevention Law provides a precedent for reform, the persistence of such provisions in other countries indicates that continued advocacy and evidence generation are needed. We call for systematic repeal of leprosy-specific immigration restrictions and for further research to complete the global map of these provisions.
