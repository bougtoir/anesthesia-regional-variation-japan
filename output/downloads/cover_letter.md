**RE: Submission of a scoping review manuscript — "Leprosy as a Ground for Labour Visa Restrictions: A Scoping Review of National Immigration Regulations Worldwide"**

On behalf of my co-authors, I am pleased to submit our manuscript entitled *"Leprosy as a Ground for Labour Visa Restrictions: A Scoping Review of National Immigration Regulations Worldwide"* for consideration for publication in *PLOS Neglected Tropical Diseases*.

**Why this manuscript is suitable for PLOS Neglected Tropical Diseases**

PLOS NTDs explicitly lists leprosy (Hansen's disease) among the major bacterial infections within its scope, and the journal's mission includes addressing public health and policy dimensions of NTDs. Our manuscript directly aligns with this mandate by examining how immigration laws worldwide regulate the entry of persons affected by leprosy—a neglected tropical disease that, despite being curable and of extremely low contagiousness, remains a ground for visa refusal in several countries. This regulatory landscape has received virtually no systematic scholarly attention, and our review fills this critical gap at the intersection of NTD policy, human rights, and migration health.

**How this study will inspire the NTD community and drive understanding**

To our knowledge, this is the first systematic scoping review to map labour visa medical requirements for leprosy across all UN member states. Our findings reveal that at least 12 countries currently maintain explicit statutory or regulatory provisions restricting labour visa or residence access for persons affected by leprosy. These countries span the Middle East (Saudi Arabia, Qatar, Kuwait, United Arab Emirates, Bahrain, Oman), Africa (South Africa), East Asia (Taiwan), Southeast Asia (Thailand, Philippines), Europe (Russia), and North America (United States). The Gulf Cooperation Council's unified medical examination regulations alone subject millions of migrant workers across six countries to mandatory leprosy screening.

These persisting restrictions are difficult to reconcile with (1) WHO's position that leprosy is of extremely low infectivity with established curative multidrug therapy, (2) the International Health Regulations (2005), which do not list leprosy as a notifiable or quarantinable disease, and (3) international human rights principles prohibiting discrimination based on health status. The social stigma that these regulations reinforce may discourage timely diagnosis and treatment seeking, undermining NTD elimination goals.

Primary-source verification was achieved for the key legal instruments analysed, including South Africa's Immigration Regulations Government Gazette No. 37679 (272 pp.), the GCC Unified Medical Examination Regulations (79 pp.), Thailand's Ministerial Regulation No. 14 B.E. 2535, Taiwan's CDC Foreigners' Health Checklist, the Philippines' SIRV medical form, the United States' CDC Technical Instructions for Panel Physicians, and Russia's Federal Law No. 357-FZ.

By providing the first comprehensive global map of such provisions, this review offers an evidence base for advocacy toward the removal of medically unjustified immigration restrictions affecting persons with leprosy—a population already vulnerable to stigma and marginalization.
